The Sun

The Sun is a massive yellow dwarf star at the heart of our Solar System, holding more than 99.8% of its total mass. Its energy is produced by nuclear fusion, converting hydrogen into helium and releasing immense amounts of heat and light. This energy drives weather, climates, and ecosystems on Earth. The Sun's strong gravity keeps planets, moons, comets, and asteroids in orbit. Its surface, called the photosphere, reaches about 5,500¬∞C, while the core is millions of degrees hot. Solar flares and coronal mass ejections can affect Earth's space environment, sometimes disrupting satellites and communications.

Mercury is the smallest planet and the closest to the Sun, orbiting once every 88 days. With virtually no atmosphere, it cannot retain heat, resulting in extreme surface temperatures ranging from scorching hot during the day to freezing cold at night. The surface is covered with craters, resembling Earth‚Äôs Moon, because it lacks geological activity to renew itself. Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known world to support life. About 71% of its surface is covered by water, which plays a crucial role in regulating climate and sustaining ecosystems. Its atmosphere, composed mainly of nitrogen and oxygen, provides breathable air and shields the planet from harmful solar radiation. Mars

Mars, the Red Planet, is the fourth planet from the Sun and has long fascinated humans with the possibility of past or present life. Its reddish color comes from iron oxide (rust) on its surface. Mars has the tallest volcano, Olympus Mons, and the deepest canyon, Valles Marineris, in the Solar System. Its atmosphere is thin and mostly carbon dioxide, making it cold and unable to support liquid water on the surface for long. However, evidence suggests ancient rivers, lakes, and possibly oceans once existed. Mars has two small irregular moons, Phobos and Deimos, and is the target of numerous exploration missions.

Saturn
